Materials Testing & Experimental Design

3D-printed cancellous bone structures: characterizing strength, stiffness, and porosity for implant development.

Traditional bone implants made from stainless steel or titanium are far stiffer than the natural bone they replace, causing stress shielding and long-term bone loss. Our research investigated whether 3D-printed porous geometries could better replicate the mechanical behavior and nutrient flow characteristics of cancellous bone. We designed and fabricated specimens across two infill geometries and two densities, then put them through tensile testing, compression testing, and a custom porosity measurement, extracting elastic moduli and flow rates from every configuration.

Project Scope

16 tensile tests, 12 compression tests, and a custom porosity study across four specimen configurations.

We used polylactic acid (PLA) and a Prusa i3 MK3S+ to print dogbone and cylinder specimens in two infill geometries, gyroid and 3D cross, at 25% and 35% infill density. Every specimen was tested on an Instron machine to generate force-displacement data that we converted into stress-strain curves using MATLAB. The elastic modulus for each sample was extracted from a linear fit applied to the first 4% of strain.

Beyond mechanical testing, we designed a gravity-fed water flow experiment to rank the porosity and permeability of each geometry, measuring the time for 80% of 100 mL to pass through each specimen. This gave us a direct experimental window into how well each structure would support nutrient flow in a real biological context.

Background

Why cancellous bone is so difficult to replicate with conventional implants

Human bone is composed of two tissue types. Cortical bone forms the dense outer shell and provides primary structural support. Cancellous bone, also called spongy bone, forms the internal lattice. Its porous architecture stores bone marrow, supports red blood cell production through erythropoiesis, aids in mineral regulation, and crucially allows nutrients and biological fluids to flow freely through the structure.

Conventional implant materials like stainless steel and titanium are orders of magnitude stiffer than cancellous bone. This stiffness mismatch causes a phenomenon called stress shielding: the implant bears so much of the applied load that the surrounding bone receives insufficient mechanical stimulus, leading to gradual bone resorption and implant loosening over time. Beyond stiffness, these solid metals have essentially zero porosity, blocking the nutrient flow that healthy bone depends on.

Our research question was direct: can a 3D-printed porous polymer geometry, selected for both its geometry and its infill density, produce a combination of stiffness and permeability that more closely matches natural cancellous bone? We chose gyroid and 3D cross infill patterns as our two geometries because they represent meaningfully different internal architectures and are both achievable at small scale with standard FDM printing.

Why gyroid versus 3D cross

The gyroid is a triply periodic minimal surface with a naturally isotropic internal structure. Stresses distribute more evenly across the geometry rather than concentrating at specific nodes or intersections. The 3D cross, by contrast, creates a more directional lattice with clear stress concentration points at crossings. Our hypothesis was that the gyroid's isotropic nature would produce higher elastic moduli and faster fluid flow at equivalent infill densities.

PLA as a proxy material

We selected PLA because of its compatibility with our FDM printer and its well-documented mechanical behavior. We acknowledged upfront that PLA is not a viable long-term implant material: it degrades in biological environments and exhibits viscoelastic properties that real bone implant materials would not. Our specimens served as geometric proxies, not material substitutes. The goal was to characterize how geometry and density affect mechanical response, independent of the specific material.

Theory

Stress, strain, and the modulus of elasticity

The Instron machine outputs raw force and displacement data. Converting those into material properties requires a stress-strain analysis built on three equations.

Stress

Stress σ is the force applied per unit cross-sectional area. For tensile specimens we measured the width and thickness of the reduced area with calipers before each test. For compression cylinders we measured the diameter.

Engineering stress σ = F / A

F = applied force (N), A = original cross-sectional area (m²)

Strain

Strain ε is the fractional change in length relative to the original specimen length. For tensile tests, the reference length was the reduced area region of the dogbone. We used engineering strain throughout, keeping the reference geometry fixed.

Engineering strain ε = l / l₀

l = displacement, l₀ = original gauge length

Elastic modulus

The modulus of elasticity E is the slope of the linear (elastic) region of the stress-strain curve, representing the material's stiffness. For metals this region is easy to identify. For PLA with a porous geometry, the transition from elastic to plastic behavior is gradual, so we defined the linear region as the first 1 mm of displacement, corresponding to approximately 4% strain. We applied a linear least-squares fit to that range to extract E.

Elastic modulus E = σ / ε

Computed as the slope of the stress-strain line of best fit over 0 to 4% strain

Uncertainty propagation

We applied Gauss's Law of error propagation to quantify uncertainty in stress, strain, and modulus. Uncertainty in stress for tensile specimens depended on force measurement uncertainty and the two caliper measurements (width and thickness). For compression specimens it depended on force and the single diameter measurement. Uncertainty in modulus compounded both stress and strain uncertainties through equation 7 in the report. Typical modulus uncertainties were on the order of 10⁷ Pa, roughly 5 to 15% of the measured value depending on the configuration.

Sample Preparation

Designing specimens for meaningful Instron data

Tensile specimens: ASTM D638 dogbones

Tensile test specimens were designed to the ASTM D638 standard for non-reinforced plastics. The dogbone shape concentrates stress in a reduced cross-section at the center of the specimen, ensuring fracture occurs there rather than at the grips. All specimens were printed at a 0.15 mm layer height. The first layer printed at 5 mm/s for adhesion, and all subsequent layers printed at 30 mm/s. We prepared four specimens per configuration: four gyroid 25%, four gyroid 35%, four 3D cross 25%, and four 3D cross 35%.

A complication we encountered was that some dogbones fractured near the gripping section rather than at the reduced area. This happened because stress concentrations formed at the grip contact zone, likely due to over-tightening or layer misalignment near the ends of the print. Data from those samples was excluded from final analysis. We redesigned those areas in subsequent prints to prevent recurrence.

Compression specimens: open-wall cylinders

Compression specimens were cylindrical rather than rectangular to minimize edge stress concentrations. A critical design decision was removing the solid outer wall that FDM printers normally add around the perimeter of a print. We discovered during early tests that cylinders with solid walls showed modulus values dominated by the wall rather than by the interior infill geometry, which was what we actually wanted to characterize. Reprinting without outer walls resolved this and made the compression data reflect the true porous structure. The same cylinder geometry was reused for porosity testing.

Porosity specimens

The compression cylinders doubled as porosity test specimens. We placed each cylinder inside a 1-inch tube, poured 100 mL of water through it, and recorded the scale reading as a function of time using a video and stopwatch. The reported metric was the time for 80 g (80%) of the water to pass through, giving a practical flow rate ranking across configurations.

Results: Tensile Testing

Gyroid outperforms 3D cross in tensile stiffness at every density

Sixteen tensile tests were completed. For each configuration we plotted force vs. displacement, computed stress and strain, and extracted the elastic modulus from a linear fit over the first 4% of strain. The results confirmed our hypothesis consistently across all four groups.

2.31 × 10⁸ Pa Gyroid 25% tensile modulus
2.84 × 10⁸ Pa Gyroid 35% tensile modulus
1.99 × 10⁸ Pa 3D Cross 25% tensile modulus
2.45 × 10⁸ Pa 3D Cross 35% tensile modulus

Two trends held across all configurations. First, higher infill density produced a higher modulus for both geometries. More material per unit volume directly increases stiffness. Second, the gyroid always outperformed the 3D cross at the same density. Gyroid 25% had a higher tensile modulus than 3D Cross 35%, demonstrating that geometry matters as much as density: a sparser gyroid is stiffer in tension than a denser cross lattice.

The physical explanation is the gyroid's isotropic stress distribution. When a tensile force is applied, the gyroid's continuous curved surface distributes the load more uniformly across the cross section. The 3D cross concentrates stress at node intersections, creating local weak points where failure initiates at lower average stresses.

Results: Compression Testing

The same geometry advantage holds under compression, with a larger performance gap

Twelve compression tests were completed. The same modulus extraction method was used, with the cutoff shifted to 1 mm of displacement (rather than 4% strain) to define the linear region, because the zero-force calibration required eliminating data below 5 N to account for machine contact uncertainty.

2.11 × 10⁸ Pa Gyroid 25% compression modulus
3.22 × 10⁸ Pa Gyroid 35% compression modulus
7.77 × 10⁷ Pa 3D Cross 25% compression modulus
1.33 × 10⁸ Pa 3D Cross 35% compression modulus

The performance gap between geometries is larger under compression than under tension. Gyroid 25% compressive modulus (2.11 × 10⁸ Pa) is nearly three times higher than 3D Cross 25% (7.77 × 10⁷ Pa). This is consistent with the isotropic argument: under compression, the gyroid distributes compressive load evenly across its curved surface, while the 3D cross resists compression primarily through its vertical members, with the horizontal crossbars contributing less effectively. That architectural mismatch amplifies under compressive loading.

Both geometries followed the same density trend seen in tension: higher infill led to a higher compressive modulus. No exceptions were observed across either geometry or test type. The density effect and the geometry effect were independent and additive.

Results: Porosity

Gyroid allows nearly 8× faster fluid flow than the equivalent cross structure

The porosity study measured the time for 80% of a 100 mL water column to pass through each specimen. Shorter times mean higher permeability and more open internal pathways, both of which are beneficial for nutrient delivery in a bone implant context.

2.02 s Gyroid 25% flow time
4.53 s Gyroid 35% flow time
13.02 s 3D Cross 25% flow time
16.66 s 3D Cross 35% flow time

The gyroid at 25% infill allowed water to pass through in 2.02 seconds. The 3D cross at 25% infill took 13.02 seconds, more than six times longer. At 35% infill, the gap narrows slightly but remains dramatic: 4.53 seconds for gyroid versus 16.66 seconds for 3D cross.

This is the isotropic structure effect again, now expressed in fluid dynamics rather than solid mechanics. The gyroid's interconnected curved channels provide continuous, evenly distributed flow paths in all directions. Water encounters no preferential dead ends or narrow bottlenecks. The 3D cross, with its more rectilinear channel structure, forces fluid through narrower passages and creates more tortuous paths. The result is slower, less uniform flow.

Notably, both geometries followed the expected trend where lower infill density (more open pores) produced faster flow. This trend held consistently across both geometries. The gyroid's advantage was not just in flow speed but in the ratio of mechanical stiffness to permeability: it achieves higher modulus and higher flow rate simultaneously, which is exactly the combination a bone implant needs.

We acknowledge that this porosity method was preliminary. A proper permeability measurement would use Darcy's Law, requiring controlled pressure measurements and a calibrated flow meter. Our time-based gravity flow test establishes relative rankings but not absolute permeability values. Future work would integrate a flow meter and pressure sensors to produce quantitative permeability data using the Darcy relation Q = kA(ΔP / μL).

Conclusions

Gyroid geometry is the superior choice across all three metrics tested

Across 28 mechanical tests and four porosity measurements, the gyroid infill geometry outperformed the 3D cross in every category: tensile modulus, compressive modulus, and fluid permeability. Higher infill density consistently improved mechanical stiffness for both geometries. The geometry effect and the density effect were both real and independent of one another.

The practical implication for implant design is that geometry selection is as important as density selection, and the gyroid's isotropic continuous surface provides structural and functional advantages that a directional lattice cannot match. A gyroid structure at lower infill density can match or exceed the stiffness of a 3D cross at higher density while maintaining substantially better porosity, a meaningful tradeoff for biocompatibility.

The primary limitation of this work is PLA as a material. Its viscoelasticity complicates the identification of a clean elastic region on the stress-strain curve, and it is not a viable long-term implant material. The next step would be repeating this study with biocompatible polymers or ceramic composites that better match the stiffness range of native cancellous bone, combined with the more rigorous Darcy's Law porosity methodology outlined above.
